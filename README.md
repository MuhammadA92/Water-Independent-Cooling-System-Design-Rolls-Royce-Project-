# Heat Rejection System Modelling & Optimisation (Rolls-Royce Project)

## Overview
This project involves the development of a water-independent heat rejection system for power generation applications in water-scarce environments, in collaboration with Rolls-Royce. The focus is on designing and evaluating scalable cooling solutions through computational modelling and engineering analysis.

The project was part of a multi-university programme involving over 20 teams from institutions including Imperial College London, UCL, Oxford, and Cambridge, where our team placed 3rd overall.

## My Role
I contributed specifically to the heat rejection subsystem, working on both the development of the final computational model and a separate optimisation tool for system design.

My work focused on translating engineering principles into code, enabling data-driven evaluation of system performance and design decisions.

## Technical Implementation

### Heat Rejection Modelling
I contributed to the development of the final system model by implementing engineering calculations and thermodynamic relationships in code to simulate heat rejection performance.

This involved translating theoretical equations into a computational framework, allowing system behaviour to be analysed under varying conditions.

### Pipe Configuration Optimisation
A key component of my work was developing an optimisation model for a stacked pipe heat exchanger design.

- Modelled a system where multiple pipes are arranged within a fixed volume constraint  
- Analysed how increasing pipe count reduces individual pipe diameter  
- Evaluated trade-offs between increased surface area and increased frictional losses  

The optimisation tool:
- Took inputs such as pipe length, friction coefficients, and flow parameters  
- Incorporated fluid dynamics considerations including flow resistance and pressure losses  
- Determined the most efficient pipe configuration for overall system performance  

This required balancing competing factors such as flow efficiency, thermal performance, and physical constraints.

## Systems & Engineering Approach
The project emphasised system-level thinking, where performance could not be optimised along a single dimension. Instead, solutions required balancing:

- Thermal efficiency  
- Flow behaviour and frictional losses  
- Cost and feasibility  
- Physical and geometric constraints  

A significant part of the work involved structured trade-off analysis to identify the most effective overall system design.
